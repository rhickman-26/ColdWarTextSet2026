The Architecture of Containment (1945-1960)

Setting the Stage: A World Divided

World War II is over, but there is no peace. The United States and the Soviet Union—former allies—are now bitter enemies. America is afraid that the Soviets will spread communism across the globe. To stop this, U.S. leaders decide they must "contain" (stop) communism wherever it appears, leading to fear at home and new wars abroad.

Containment: The U.S. policy to stop the spread of communism.
Iron Curtain: The imaginary line dividing democratic West Europe from communist East Europe.
Red Scare: The intense fear that communists were hiding inside the U.S. government.
Mission Briefing: SSUSH20

We are learning to analyze how U.S. foreign and domestic policies responded to the threat of communism during the Truman and Eisenhower administrations so that we can understand how early Cold War decisions shaped American global leadership and domestic culture.

  • I can explain how the Truman Doctrine and containment established America's role as the leader of the free world.
  • I can describe how the Marshall Plan rebuilt Europe and expanded American influence.
  • I can analyze how the Korean War demonstrated America's commitment to containing communism globally.
  • I can identify how McCarthyism shaped American domestic culture and civil liberties.
Harry S. Truman1945-1953
Democrat
Presidency SummaryAssumed office after FDR's death. He oversaw the end of WWII (dropping the atomic bombs), the desegregation of the military, and the "Fair Deal" domestic agenda.

Dwight D. Eisenhower1953-1961
Republican
Presidency SummaryA WWII 5-Star General. His "Dynamic Conservatism" brought the Interstate Highway System and economic prosperity to 1950s America.
Cold War RoleThe General. Ended the Korean War; formulated the "Domino Theory"; authorized the U-2 spy plane missions; warned against the "Military-Industrial Complex."

Crises, Space, & Vietnam (1960-1968)

Setting the Stage: On the Brink

The Cold War gets dangerous. The U.S. and Soviet Union race to build more nuclear weapons and compete to see who can get to space first. Tensions are so high that one wrong move could destroy the world. Meanwhile, America sends thousands of soldiers into the jungles of Vietnam to fight communism, but the war becomes unpopular at home.

Space Race: The competition between the US and USSR to achieve spaceflight capability.
Domino Theory: The belief that if one nation fell to communism, its neighbors would follow.
Sputnik: The first artificial satellite, launched by the Soviets, which shocked America.
Mission Briefing: SSUSH21

We are learning to evaluate how the Kennedy and Johnson administrations navigated Cold War crises, including the Space Race and the Vietnam War, so that we can understand how superpower competition influenced American foreign policy and military involvement.

  • I can explain the significance of the Space Race, Sputnik, and the moon landing.
  • I can analyze the causes and consequences of the Cuban Missile Crisis.
  • I can evaluate the causes of U.S. escalation in the Vietnam War.
  • I can analyze the role of the media in shaping public opinion during the Vietnam War.
John F. Kennedy1961-1963
Democrat
Presidency SummaryYoungest elected president. Proposed the "New Frontier" domestic program and Peace Corps. His presidency was cut short by assassination in Dallas, 1963.
Cold War RoleCrisis Manager. Humiliated by the Bay of Pigs; pledged to land a man on the moon; successfully navigated the Cuban Missile Crisis; negotiated Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty.
Lyndon B. Johnson1963-1969
Democrat
Presidency SummarySworn in after JFK's death. Launched the "Great Society" (War on Poverty, Medicare) and signed the landmark Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965.
Cold War RoleWartime President. Escalated U.S. involvement in Vietnam following the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, which eventually overshadowed his domestic achievements.

Gallery 3

Détente to Dissolution (1969-1991)

Setting the Stage: The Long Road to Peace

After decades of fear, things begin to change. President Nixon tries a new strategy called "Détente" to calm things down. He even visits communist China! But by the 1980s, President Reagan challenges the Soviet Union to tear down its walls. The Soviet economy crumbles, and the Cold War finally comes to an end.

Détente: A period of easing tensions and better relations between the US and USSR.
Triangular Diplomacy: Nixon's strategy to pit communist China and the Soviet Union against each other.
War Powers Act: A law passed to stop the President from starting wars without Congress's permission.
Mission Briefing: SSUSH22-23

We are learning to analyze the shift in American foreign policy from the Vietnam era through the collapse of the Soviet Union so that we can understand how changing leadership strategies impacted the end of the Cold War.

  • I can explain the strategic impact of Nixon's opening of diplomatic relations with China.
  • I can describe the purpose and significance of the War Powers Act.
  • I can analyze how Reagan's economic and military policies contributed to the collapse of the Soviet Union.
Richard Nixon1969-1974
Republican
Presidency SummaryEstablished the EPA and signed the Clean Air Act. His presidency ended in resignation due to the Watergate scandal, overshadowing his foreign policy achievements.
Cold War RoleThe Strategist. Pursued "Détente" (easing tensions); opened diplomatic relations with China to pressure the Soviets; signed the Paris Peace Accords ending US involvement in Vietnam.
Ronald Reagan1981-1989
Republican
Presidency SummaryLed a "Conservative Revolution" focused on cutting taxes ("Reaganomics"), reducing regulation, and appointing conservative judges. Survived an assassination attempt in 1981.
Cold War RoleThe Challenger. Abandoned détente for "Peace through Strength"; increased military spending; supported anti-communist rebels; challenged Gorbachev to "Tear down this wall."
